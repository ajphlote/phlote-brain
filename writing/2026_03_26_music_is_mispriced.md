Music Is Mispriced

Every great investment thesis starts with a mispricing.

The mispricing in music is hiding in plain sight. The entire asset class is valued on passive listening — on streams. That's the price-setter. Fractions of a cent per play, aggregated across billions of streams, spread thin across every artist on every platform.

But that's only one use of music. And it's increasingly not the most valuable one.
Think about what a piece of music actually does. It sets emotional tone. It builds context. It enhances focus, creativity, and physical performance. It's a layer that can be added to games, videos, apps, generative content, and AI-powered experiences. It's a building block, not a finished product.

When you price a building block based only on what it costs to look at it, you've mispriced it.

The shift from listening to usage is the same repricing event that happened when music moved from physical to digital — except this time, instead of prices going down, they go up. Because usage is measurable, licensable, and scalable in ways passive listening never was.

Music that can be deployed by an AI agent, integrated into a game engine, layered into a generative video experience — that music is worth orders of magnitude more than what a streaming royalty would suggest.

The market doesn't see this yet because the streaming model has dominated the frame for so long. But we're already in the early innings of a different era.

We're acquiring catalog at listening-era prices and positioning it to monetize at usage-era rates. That spread is the investment thesis.
