The Job to Be Done

There's a question every founder gets eventually, usually from someone who's been watching from a distance: How do you stay focused when everything is moving so fast?

The honest answer is that I don't try to keep up with everything. I know my job.

My job is to make the most useful music catalog on earth. The most trusted. The most contextual. The most used. That's it. Everything else — every technology, every trend, every new model that drops — gets evaluated through that lens. Does it help me do that job better, faster, or cheaper? Then I care. Does it not? Then I don't.

This matters more right now than it probably ever has, because AI is producing a new tool every week that somebody is ready to tell you changes everything. And for a lot of founders, that's genuinely destabilizing. They start chasing the tool instead of the job.

I've watched this happen in music for the better part of a decade. Everyone pivots to whatever's loudest. Streaming. NFTs. AI generation. The pivot happens before anyone's figured out whether the new thing serves the actual job.

The reason I haven't been whipsawed is simple: I had a job before any of this showed up, and AI is helping me do that job. I'm not searching for something for AI to do. That distinction sounds small. It isn't.

When you know your job, clarity compounds. You make faster decisions. You say no to the right things. You attract people who understand what you're building without needing it re-explained.

The catalog I'm building has been in development for seven years. The tools available today — metadata automation, agentic infrastructure, programmable licensing — are finally catching up to what I've been trying to build. I didn't change the job to fit the tools. The tools arrived to serve the job.

That's the position I want to be in every year from now.
